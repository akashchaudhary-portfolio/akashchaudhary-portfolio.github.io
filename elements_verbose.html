Verbose: Speech Improvement Application

Is it possible to design an interface that can help non-native people to speak English in a more effective way? What would be the essential design requirements for helping ESL (English as second language) speakers to better express themselves in English?

In this study we find out the design requirements that are essential in developing an educational speech improvement interface that helps ESL speakers to express their intentions in a way that makes them easily intelligible. We hypothesize that teaching people speech intonations in a way actors are trained how to speak in movies following linguists' teaching principles, can help people better express their communicative intentions.



Summary

The style of English speaking among people who have English as their second language is different, which makes them difficult to understand. ESL (English as a second language) speakers tend to follow the tone structure of their first language, making their speech difficult to understand for native speakers. Rather than focusing on important information in the speech, ESL speakers focus on parts of speech which do not convey intentioanlity. For example, Hindi is a syllable-timed language and Hindi speaking people who have English as their second language (ESL), tend to speak English in a syllable-timed manner. English, however is supposed to be spoken in a stress-timed manner. This limits the opportunities for education and employment among ESL speakers.

We build an interactive smartphone-based educational mobile application using the user-centered design process. Through 4 iterative user testing phases, we discover 8 design parameters that are necessary for developing such an application. This application teaches English intonations based on globally consistent pitch patterns through conversations with a trained chat assistant, which inculcates expert linguists’ teaching principles. After co-designing the application’s parameters with primary stakeholders and expert visual designers, we assess its effectiveness by measuring the pre and post-performance of the users after the system usage, using various quantitative measures, like intonation scores, SEQ, and SUS.

Results and feedback from users on the final mobile application provides evidence that ESL speakers found the app to be usable and were better able to express their intentions with the use of such an application.People who used our application found signifcant improvement in the perception of their vocal expressions, thereby highlighting the necessity of such a system in improving the quality of conversations that people have in general. Our study has positive implications in terms of use cases afforded to people to help them become better public speakers, which has direct relationship to personal and professional growth in terms of fostering meaningful relationships.

Literature Review

We wanted to find out what kinds of techniques native speakers use to embed intentionality and expressiveness in speech.

Along with discovering other paralinguistic parameteres which have been used conventionally to embed stress in speech, like pitch, amplitude, silences, etc, we also discovered 4 intonations in English which are used to express pragmatic intentions in communicative expressions. These intonations are also taught in film schools to teach why, when and how to pronounce certain parts of speech.

We use these 4 intonations as our prerequisite design requirement that are necessary to help people practice English speaking in an effective manner.




Cue Card Testing

We wanted to test the validity of the 4 intonations in terms of the effect they have on user speech.

As a proof of concept study, we designed a mock conversation set-up supposed to be enacted between 2 people which visually showed where and how to intoante. We tested this on 10 participants, each participant performing the conversation with a researcher.

We obtained positive quantitative results of user performance on intonations used by study participants. Further, user interviews revealed that the use of intonations through dialogues helped them in practicing contextual speech expressions. This gave us two intitial design requirements (R1, R2) for our mobile application.

Low-Fidelity Testing Using Sketch-based Wireframes

We wanted to come up with the basic app structure, find useful interactions that aide learning, curate relevant information to be displayed, and figure out the complete application flow.

We performed a talk aloud user study on 10 particiapnts and asked them to go through the entire app structure while brainstorming about it on the go.

The tests revealed 5 new design requirements to be embedded in the final mobile application design.



Design Requirements From Low Fidelity Study



Rx DESIGN DESCRIPTION
R3 Introductory/concluding screens Introductory screen presents conceptual facts on intonations and concluding screen rationalizes why to use a particular intonation (7/10)
R4 Gradual increase in difficulty Gradual increase in number of intonations and difficulty of a training sentence enable ease of learning (10/10)

Visual Representation Testing

For displaying how the intonations in English language are supposed to be spoken, we wanted to find an intuitive textual design for representing pitch in user speech which would be embedded in our final mobile application.

After consulting with a design expert, we came up with an exhaustive list of 12 visual representation designs by combining all possible permutations of --- highlighting a text (using italics, underline, bold, text color, background color) in a standard text editor, and highlighting rise and fall of pitch (using text size variation and line graphs). We tested these visual representation designs with 10 students in a visual design class.

This helped us in coming up with another design requirement (R8) for representing audio information visually (which uses bold and text size variation) in the final mobile application.


Although a universal agreement has been reached to represent all possible human speech sounds through International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA), little consensus been has developed for the representation of stress or pitch modulation. The figure represents the various ways in which pitch has been represented textually historically. A) depicts the notation presented by James Rush, B) presents the notation given by Lieberman tracing the 10th harmonic of a spoken sentence, C) depicts notation presented by Crystal, D) presents the notation presented by Bolinger, and E) portrays the notation presented by Ladefoged.

High-Fidelity Testing on Final Mobile Application

Including the 8 design requirements obtained through iterative testing from our previous user studies, we wanted to develop and test our final mobile application for usability, user experience and qualitative insights.

We designed and built our mobile application on an iOS mobile platform. We used Google server for transcribing user audio and Praat software for extracting pitch. We designed our own algorithm at the backend to analyze pitch values of users in the mobile application.

User testing of our application revealed more than average usability score (SUS - 82.3/100), "good" rating on user experience (UEQ) and various user insights validating our 8 design requirements.

Mobile Application Testing


A comprehensive user evaluation of the mobile application across the 4 phases --- pre-training test, training, post-training test and analysis, revealed various quantitative and qualitative insights that helped us validate the effectiveness of our design requirements in teaching English intonations to ESL speakers for improving their speech expressions in terms of affording better communicative intentions. We conducted the study with each participant over 3 sessions, i.e., we conducted the study over 3 days to reveal longitudinal effects of learning.



SEQ (Single Ease Questionnaire)


A Single Ease Questionnaire (SEQ) was condcutedd uring session 1: one immediately after Pre-Training test, second after completing the Training, third after the Post-Training test and fourth after going through the Analysis section. The mean SEQ rating for the four phases --- Pre-Training, Training, Post-Training and Analysis section came out to be --- 6.06, 4.31, 5.12, and 6.25 (out of 7).

Since users were made to learn about new intonations, and why, how, and where to apply them in the training section, it was expected for this section to have a higher cognitive load for the users as compared to the other sections. A 4.31 rating (somewhere between average and somewhat easy), therefore seems justifable.


UEQ (User Experience Questionnaire)


A UEQ questionnaire revealed user reponses on features of the app involving attractiveness, perspicuity, efficiency, dependability, stimulation and novelty.



SUS (System Usability Scale)

The SUS scores were used to find the app usability. Any score above 80.3 (out of 100) on the SUS scale is among the top 10 percentile and correlates to the adjective rating of good. The overall user-friendliness of our app was rated 82.3 (out of 100) on SUS. The score was much above the required average benchmark score on SUS, which is 68. These results proved the hypothesis of our app in teaching the requisite English speaking skills. Quantitative analysis of learning gain across different intonation and across sessions revealed each result was statistically significant. Further, the percentage incorrect performance of the users was used to find the level of difficulty of each intonation. To further validate our findings and ground them in reason, these numbers were assisted from our qualitative findings of semi structured interviews conducted after session3 of the study.



Qualitative Observations

Interviews revealed user insights that highlight certain aspects of the mobile application that they found helpful in improving speech expressions.

Novelty of the system

''I was unaware and didn’t think specifcally about stress (intonations) till now. I always thought more on clarity or time spent on a topic, rather than the stress in the pitch.''

Gamification

''The design of the training exercises was very effective in my opinion. The screens were very interactive, it almost became like a challenge not to enter in the Bonus section and get the intonation right in the given number of tries.''

Visual Representation

''...(the context) was easy to understand because of the changed characters (in the visual representation)''

Conceptual Facts on Intonations

''The introductory screen was very important as it introduced me with new conceptual knowledge, which made things relatively easy to understand when I had to perform in the exercises. Also, the feedback screen gave explanations that why depending on a particular question (context) asked by Verbose, the intonation of an answer for specifc words will change.''

Effectiveness of teaching pedagogy

''I guess it (the mobile application) provides hints towards the modulation of how you speak. If someone is talking really fast, or if someone doesn’t know what he or she is speaking, just by the modulation you can tell if that sentence is a question or a statement.''

Gradual Increase in Difficulty

''When the session fnishes, it seems like you have learned a lot. It is a lot of new things, but I think the content has been designed intelligently. I didn’t feel anytime that I have to learn a lot suddenly. It was a gradual process.''

Utility of the Mobile Application

''You can’t understand the formation of sentences in their (ESL speakers) speech, so you are in general confused (about context) what they are trying to stress on. At those moments you understand that it (context specifc stress words) matters...like some people intonate words like "the" and "it" , and it just makes the sentence confusing to interpret.''
''It’s important because you want to convey the intention very clearly...my speech has improved. I am not a bland speaker anymore (laughs).''
